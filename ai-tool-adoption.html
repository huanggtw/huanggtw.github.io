Mixed Methods · Client Project

Professional Identity & AI Tool Adoption

Work, Identity & Wellbeing Lab  ·  the research team at a large academic medical center
Advised by Dr. Mari Kira  ·  Ongoing

A qualitative study examining how an AI clinical transcription tool affects physicians' professional identity and daily workflows at a large academic medical center. The study compared active users who continued using the tool with lapsed users who abandoned it, to understand what drives adoption and what causes drop-off.

The Brief

When a tool meant to help starts to get in the way.

A large academic medical center introduced an AI clinical transcription tool designed to reduce documentation burden and free up time for patient care. The research team wanted to understand how physicians were actually experiencing it, not just whether they were using it. They brought in the Work, Identity and Wellbeing Lab to look deeper, examining how the tool was shaping the way physicians see themselves and their work.

Physician burnout is a well-documented crisis in American healthcare, and administrative burden is one of its leading drivers. Tools like this represent a growing wave of AI interventions designed to address that burden. What is less understood is how these tools affect not just efficiency, but identity: how physicians understand their role, their expertise, and their relationship with patients when a machine is sitting between them.

My Role

Analysis and consultation, not data collection.

The research team handled recruitment and interviews. My contribution began where the data collection ended. Working from 42 interview transcripts across two participant groups, I was responsible for making sense of what physicians were actually saying, and what it meant.

This was an unusual position to be in. I had not been in the room for any of the interviews. I had not heard the pauses, the hesitations, or the moments where a physician's tone shifted. I was working entirely from text. That constraint shaped how I approached the analysis. I had to be especially rigorous about grounding every interpretation in the transcript itself rather than in assumptions about what the conversation must have felt like.

Beyond the analysis, I delivered a consultation to the research team on how to strengthen their interview protocol for future research, specifically around how to surface professional identity as a theme more directly, since it was central to how physicians were experiencing the tool but rarely named explicitly.

Methods

One dataset. Two very different stories.

The 42 transcripts split into two distinct groups, each requiring its own analytical lens: 31 from active users who had continued using the tool, and 11 from lapsed users who had tried it and stopped. Analyzing them together would have flattened the contrast. Analyzing them separately allowed the differences to surface on their own terms.

1

Reflexive Thematic Analysis in NVivo

Primary method · 42 transcripts

I used reflexive thematic analysis, an approach that treats the researcher's perspective as part of the analytical process rather than something to be eliminated. Rather than coding toward a predetermined framework, I read through all 42 transcripts multiple times before beginning to code, building an intuitive sense of the data before imposing any structure on it. Themes were developed iteratively, revisited, and refined as patterns emerged and contradictions surfaced. NVivo allowed me to manage the volume of data systematically while keeping the analysis interpretive rather than mechanical. I used it to track how themes evolved across transcripts, compare patterns between the two groups, and ensure that every theme was grounded in specific textual evidence.

Journey Mapping

Supporting method · 2 user groups

In parallel with the thematic analysis, I developed journey maps for each user group, tracing the arc of their experience with the tool from initial expectations through adoption or abandonment. Journey mapping is typically used in UX research to visualize a user's path through a product or service. Applied to qualitative interview data, it became a way of understanding not just what physicians thought about the tool, but when and why their relationship with it changed. The maps were also a practical output, a way of translating dense qualitative findings into a format that the research team could use directly in conversations with stakeholders and product teams.

Analytical Challenges

What made this analysis hard.

Reading without hearing

Interview transcripts lose a great deal in translation. Tone, hesitation, emphasis, and emotion are all flattened into text. A sentence that reads as neutral on the page might have been said with frustration or resignation in the room. I had to be deliberate about not over-interpreting, staying close to what was actually written rather than projecting meaning that might not have been there.

Two groups, one codebook

Analyzing active and lapsed users separately meant building two parallel analytical frameworks that still needed to speak to each other. Themes that appeared in both groups often meant very different things depending on which group they came from. Staying precise about those distinctions, rather than collapsing them into a single narrative, was one of the more demanding parts of the work.

Professional identity as an unnamed theme

The most significant analytical challenge was that professional identity was central to how physicians were experiencing the tool, but almost no one named it directly. Physicians described feeling like editors rather than clinicians, or losing their voice in their own notes, without framing these as identity experiences. Surfacing that theme required reading carefully for what was implied rather than stated, and being rigorous about the distinction between interpretation and projection.

Key Themes

What the analysis revealed, at a high level.

The analysis surfaced a consistent pattern across both groups: whether physicians continued using the tool or abandoned it depended largely on how well it fit into how they already worked and thought about themselves as clinicians. For active users, the benefits were real but came with trade-offs. For lapsed users, the costs consistently outweighed the benefits. In both cases, the tool's impact extended well beyond workflow into how physicians understood their professional role.
