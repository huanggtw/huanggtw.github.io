Coursework · PSYCH 303

Structured Rubrics
& Hiring Bias

University of Michigan · Department of Psychology · PSYCH 303 · Fall 2024

Experimental Design Qualtrics Amazon MTurk Independent t-Tests Between-Subjects
Overview

Hiring rubrics are supposed to make hiring fairer, but almost nobody tests whether they actually work. For my final project in PSYCH 303, I ran an experiment to find out. Participants stepped into the role of a hiring committee member reviewing three fictional candidates for a marketing job, some with a structured rubric in hand and some without. I expected the rubric to sand down bias and bring people's decisions closer together. Instead, it changed almost nothing, and figuring out why turned out to be the more interesting project.

01
Research Question

Everyone agrees bias exists. Fewer people test the fixes

It's well established that hiring bias is real. Studies have shown that identical resumes get noticeably fewer callbacks when the applicant has a distinctively Black name instead of a white one. What's talked about far less is whether the tools companies actually use, like structured rubrics, do anything to change that. I wanted to test one of the most common suggested fixes directly instead of taking it on faith, so I built a rubric and put it up against no rubric at all.

Literature Review Hiring Bias
02
Study Design

Putting people in the hiring seat

I told participants they were joining a hiring committee for BrightWave Marketing, a fictional agency looking to fill a Digital Marketing Specialist role, and had everyone review the same three candidates. Half the group got a rubric covering qualifications, experience, and skill diversity to reference the whole time. The other half just went with their gut. That was the only thing I let differ between the two groups, so any gap in how they judged candidates could be traced back to the rubric itself.

Experimental Design Random Assignment
03
Procedure

Walking through the decision, not just the survey

Participants reviewed each candidate one at a time, rated them, made a hiring call, and wrote a short justification for it, the same way they might explain a decision to a colleague. Once all three were reviewed, I asked how fair and how easy the process had felt, and the rubric group also told me how much the rubric actually helped. Afterward, everyone learned the candidates weren't real and what the study was really testing.

Qualtrics Amazon MTurk
04
Analysis

Checking whether the rubric moved anything at all

I combined each candidate's ratings into a single score and compared the rubric and no-rubric groups statistically, then did the same for their hiring recommendations and how confident they felt in their decisions. Across every candidate and every measure, the two groups landed in the same place. The rubric wasn't quietly nudging people, it simply wasn't showing up in the data.

29 participants took part. Nothing, not ratings, recommendations, or confidence, differed meaningfully between the rubric and no-rubric groups for any of the three candidates.

Independent t-Tests Composite Scoring
05
Discussion

A null result isn't a dead end, it's a different question

A flat result doesn't mean rubrics can't reduce bias, it means this rubric, in this task, didn't. My best guess is that people without a rubric were already leaning on criteria that looked a lot like it, so there was never much room for a gap to open up. It's also possible the rubric needed to push harder against people's default instincts to actually register, and a sample of 29 wasn't going to catch anything subtle. What stuck with me most from this project wasn't the bias question, it was realizing how easy it is for an intervention to look reasonable on paper and still be too weak to do anything in practice, which is something I now check for in every study I design.
