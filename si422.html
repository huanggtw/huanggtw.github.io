Coursework · SI 422

Navigating Resources
as a Transfer Student

University of Michigan · School of Information · SI 422 · Winter 2025

Semi-Structured Interviews Affinity Diagramming Persona Development Journey Mapping
Overview

Transfer students arrive at U-M without the two or three years the rest of their class already spent absorbing where things are and who to ask. For SI 422, I led a UX needs assessment to understand what that catch-up process actually feels like: how transfer students discover and navigate academic and career resources, where the experience breaks down, and what actually helps them find their footing. I interviewed five current transfer students, then synthesized their experiences into an affinity diagram, a persona, and a journey map, translating raw interview data into user needs and concrete design opportunities.

01
Framing the Problem

A user need hiding behind an onboarding gap

Most students absorb the location of resources gradually, through orientation, roommates, and just being on campus long enough. Transfer students skip straight to the middle of that timeline without the buildup, which made this less a usability question about any single tool and more a question about the whole ecosystem of resources they were expected to navigate. I wanted to understand what filled that gap for them today, whether it was the university's own systems or something they had to piece together themselves, and where in that experience things broke down.

02
Interview Design

Iterating the guide after a rocky pilot

My first pilot interview ran long and thin. My interviewee needed more room to warm up before diving into specifics, so I added background questions about their major, year, and transfer story to ease into the conversation. I also noticed my questions about resource-seeking were too broad to produce anything concrete, so I split them into academic and career-specific versions. The last change mattered most: instead of assigning a task upfront, I started asking whether there was something they actually wanted to look up that day, only falling back to a scripted advising-appointment task if they didn't have one. That small shift made the contextual inquiry portion feel like watching someone's real behavior instead of a staged demo.

03
Synthesis

Turning five conversations into user insights

I pulled quotes from all five interviews onto sticky notes and grouped them until patterns emerged on their own, the standard affinity mapping approach for making sense of qualitative data at this scale. A few user needs surfaced again and again: students relied on word of mouth rather than any official channel to discover resources, felt overwhelmed by irrelevant information sitting on top of what they actually needed, and hit scheduling systems that looked open until they tried to book anything, all clear usability and information architecture problems. On the more hopeful side, mentorship programs and student organizations kept coming up as the thing that actually worked, a signal about where existing touchpoints were already succeeding and worth building on rather than replacing.

Word of Mouth Reliance

Most students said friends, professors, or a lucky mention were how they actually found out a resource existed.

Information Overload

Digital platforms buried the few relevant links students needed under everything else the university offers.

Scheduling Friction

Advising and appointment portals looked open at a glance, then offered nothing for weeks once students tried to book.

Mentorship as a Bright Spot

Structured mentor programs and student organizations were the one channel students consistently trusted and returned to.

04
Persona & Journey Map

Following Alex across the service experience

To make the findings actionable for design, I built a persona, Alex, a third-year Economics transfer student who is motivated but still feels like he's navigating U-M without a map. Using the persona as an anchor, I mapped his experience across five stages of the service journey: getting oriented, hunting for essential resources, trying to book academic and career support, working to build a social network, and finally leveraging opportunities with the time he has left. Charting his emotions, actions, and pain points at each stage surfaced exactly where the experience breaks down.

Stage 1 Interested, Uncertain

Getting oriented with little structured introduction to resources.

Stage 2 Overwhelmed

Hunting for essentials across cluttered, unintuitive platforms.

Stage 3 Frustrated, Discouraged

Hitting scheduling portals with no real appointment availability.

Stage 4 Lonely

Working to build a network with few transfer-specific entry points.

Stage 5 Anxious

Racing to leverage opportunities with limited time left on campus.

The emotional arc tracks the friction closely, starting interested but uncertain, dipping into overwhelmed and frustrated around digital platforms and scheduling, sliding into loneliness while searching for community, and ending anxious about how little time remains to catch up on opportunities other students had years to find.

Persona Journey Mapping
05
Recommendations

Design opportunities at each touchpoint

Each stage of the journey pointed to a different design opportunity. Early on, a single comprehensive resource guide handed to incoming transfer students would replace the scattered word-of-mouth discovery process with an actual onboarding flow. Further in, the university's scheduling portals and digital platforms need a more intuitive redesign, since students consistently described them as confusing rather than just crowded, a clear usability fix rather than a content problem. On the social side, expanding structured mentorship and networking events specifically for transfer students, and building transfer-friendly pathways into research and student organizations that currently favor students with more time on campus, would address the loneliness and shut-out feelings that showed up again and again in the journey map. None of this requires new resources so much as a better designed path to the ones that already exist.
