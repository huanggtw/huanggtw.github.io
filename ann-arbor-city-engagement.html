The Distance Between Campus and City Hall

Commissioned by the City of Ann Arbor  ·  School of Information, University of Michigan
Client: Steve Kilar, Director of Communications  ·  Completed April 2026

A multi-method study examining why University of Michigan students disengage from city systems — and what the City of Ann Arbor can do to reach them better. Delivered as a formal research report to city leadership.

The Brief

A city trying to reach students who don't know it exists.

University of Michigan (U-M) students make up a significant portion of Ann Arbor's resident population, yet their involvement with the City seems to be minimal. Students, especially out-of-state and international students, are temporary residents with distinct communication habits, tight schedules, and low baseline awareness of civic systems.

The City of Ann Arbor partnered with the U-M School of Information to understand how effectively city systems reach this population, and what would need to change to close the gap. The final report was submitted to Steve Kilar, Director of Communications, in April 2026.

The Research Question

What keeps students from engaging? And what would make them start?

These questions guided the study, each examining a different dimension of the engagement gap.

Primary Research Question

What motivates or prevents students from engaging with city government?

  • What channels do students actually rely on for parking, transportation, and emergency alerts?
  • How do students navigate city systems in their daily lives?

Methods

Three methods. One coherent picture.

Key Findings

The barrier is structural, not attitudinal.

Across all three methods, one consistent pattern emerged: students aren't disengaged because they don't care. They're disengaged because the city has never met them where they are.

  1. 1

    Temporary identity creates permanent distance

    "I'm only gonna be here for 4 years… it doesn't necessarily directly impacts me."

    Students' sense of impermanence reduced their perceived stake in civic life. City systems felt built for permanent residents, not people passing through for just few years.

  2. 2

    Students want to engage. They just don't know how.

    56.8% said "I don't know how to" — more than those who said they don't feel the need to.

    The primary barrier is access and awareness, not apathy. Students noticed city problems daily but had no clear path from observation to action. Not one single interview participant felt their issue didn't matter.

  3. 3

    Personal relevance drives action

    59.8% said roads needed the most improvement. 57.8% wanted to interact with the city about roads and infrastructure.

    Students engaged most with issues that directly affected their daily routines, such as potholes, parking, sidewalks. Abstract civic initiatives didn't land. Once students engaged with a personally relevant issue, they often came back and did it again.

  4. 4

    Students rely on signs and friends, not official channels

    83% of students who park on the street learn parking rules from signs. Only 1 used City Hall social media.

    The city communicates through websites, social media pages, and formal announcements — none of which are embedded in how students actually move through Ann Arbor. Friends and word-of-mouth filled the gap.

Recommendations

The city has the tools. It's missing the alignment.

Our recommendations focus on meeting students where they already are — not asking them to come to the city.

  1. 1

    Push through channels students already use. Share city updates via university email newsletters, Instagram, and TikTok. Increase physical signage near campus for parking and infrastructure information. Partner with UM to surface city resources during orientation.

  2. 2

    Simplify A2FixIt and close the feedback loop. Reduce form complexity, add clearer entry points, and send personal follow-ups after submissions. 41% of diary participants found A2FixIt "confusing", demonstrating that it is a structural problem, not a user problem.

  3. 3

    Lead with personal relevance. Frame city communication around issues students encounter daily — roads, parking, sidewalks — rather than abstract civic initiatives. Students who engaged with personally relevant issues came back and used city systems again.

Reflection

I wasn't just studying the users. I was one of them.

Personal takeaway

This project hit differently from my other research. I wasn't studying a population I'd observed from the outside. I was studying myself. As an international student from Hong Kong, Ann Arbor's city government had always felt like something that existed for other people — permanent residents, locals, people who planned to stay. I'd never thought to engage with it.

Going through this process, conducting interviews, analyzing survey data, sitting with diary entries from students who felt exactly the same way I did, I started seeing the city differently. Not as something irrelevant to me, but as something that had simply never met me where I was. That distinction — between unwilling and unreached — became the most important finding of the whole project.

I also came away believing this research deserves to continue. Thirteen students over two weeks is a starting point, not a conclusion. A larger, longitudinal study could tell a much richer story about how student engagement evolves over time, and whether the city's outreach actually moves the needle.
